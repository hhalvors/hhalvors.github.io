Kierkegaard in North Zealand

Day Excursion: Esrum Abbey & Gilleleje

Theme: Withdrawal, Exposure, and Existential Awakening

Departure & Return: Copenhagen
Total Duration: ~9–10 hours

Overview

This excursion stages, physically and historically, two different moments in Christianity — and in Kierkegaard’s own development:

  • Esrum Abbey → medieval monastic withdrawal
  • Gilleleje → Kierkegaard’s personal existential crisis (1835)

The day moves from institutional renunciation to individual awakening.

Schedule

8:30 — Depart Copenhagen

Bus/Train Reading (30–40 minutes)

Assigned Texts

  • Attack Upon Christendom, No. 1
    (Christianity vs. Christendom)

  • Practice in Christianity, Preface or §§1–3
    (Offense and imitation)

Students should mark passages that: - Distinguish Christianity from cultural religion
- Emphasize imitation and inwardness
- Critique institutional security

Stop 1: Esrum Abbey

10:00–12:30

Theme: Monastic Withdrawal

Esrum Abbey

Orientation (10–15 minutes)

Brief historical context:

  • Founded 1151 (Cistercian order)
  • Vows of poverty, obedience, silence
  • Communal discipline
  • Christianity prior to modern bourgeois “Christendom”

Framing Question:

Does the monastery represent authentic Christianity — or an early institutional form?

Silent Interval (20 minutes)

Location: Near Lake Esrum

Students write briefly:

  • What would it mean to take Christianity absolutely seriously here?
  • Does withdrawal intensify faith?
  • Is monastic life radical — or structured comfort?

Discussion (30–40 minutes)

Prompts:

  1. Does separation solve the problem of Christendom?
  2. Would Kierkegaard admire the monastery?
  3. Is obedience compatible with existential inwardness?
  4. Does discipline produce faith — or merely conformity?

12:30–1:30 — Lunch

Abbey café or picnic by the lake.

Stop 2: Gilleleje

2:30–4:30

Theme: Existential Awakening & Individual Decision

Gilleleje

This stop is not symbolic abstraction — it is biographical.

In August 1835, Kierkegaard stayed in Gilleleje and wrote crucial journal entries reflecting crisis, guilt, vocation, and the need to become a self.

Required Reading at the Coast

From Kierkegaard’s Journals (August 1835, Gilleleje entries):

Suggested passages:

  • “The important thing is to find a truth which is truth for me…”
  • Reflections on vocation and inward passion
  • The need to “find the idea for which I am willing to live and die”

(Use standard English edition of Journals and Papers, or relevant Danish text.)

Structure at Gilleleje

1️⃣ Walk to the Shore (15 minutes)

Emphasize: - Open horizon
- Wind and exposure
- No enclosure

2️⃣ Silent Reading (15–20 minutes)

Students read the 1835 journal entries while facing the sea.

3️⃣ Discussion (45 minutes)

Prompts:

  1. What is different in tone from the monastery?
  2. Is Kierkegaard withdrawing — or awakening?
  3. Does faith begin in institution — or crisis?
  4. What does “truth for me” mean in this setting?
  5. Is Gilleleje a moment of escape — or confrontation?

Encourage students to connect:

  • Monastic obedience
  • Individual vocation
  • Subjective truth
  • Risk

4:45 — Depart Gilleleje

Return Assignment (Due Next Morning)

1–2 pages

Does Christianity begin in structure or in existential crisis?
Compare Esrum Abbey with Kierkegaard’s Gilleleje journals.

Students must cite: - One passage from the monastery readings
- One passage from the Gilleleje journals

Pedagogical Arc

Setting Historical Meaning Kierkegaardian Question
Esrum Abbey Medieval communal renunciation Is Christianity structured withdrawal?
Gilleleje Kierkegaard’s existential crisis Is faith personal decision?

The day traces a movement:

  • From communal obedience
  • To individual vocation
  • From silence imposed
  • To silence chosen

Closing Question

End at the shoreline with:

Is Christianity something one enters —
or something one discovers when everything else falls away?

Let them sit with it before departure.
