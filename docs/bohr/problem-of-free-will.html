The Problem of Free Will and Determinism

Does the human being have free will? This is a classic philosophical problem. In modern times, after the development of mechanical physics in the 17th century, the problem has intensified, because physics provided a rich concept of what it means for something to be determined. From the 17th century onwards, a widespread view has been that all brain processes are determined. From this, it is commonly concluded that the entire stream of consciousness is also determined, and that the experience of free will is an illusion. Determinism implies that a person is not responsible for their actions.

Since Niels Bohr’s time, many philosophical treatises have been written about the free will problem, without reaching any final solution. There are “schools” that “battle” each other, and in philosophical reference works one can read again and again about what these “schools” stand for, with all the usual labels: determinism, indeterminism, necessarianism, libertarianism, compatibilism, etc. I will not go into all of this, but instead pose the question of whether a general ontological determinism is even compatible with the concept of knowledge. If everything in the universe, including all human behavior and consciousness, is determined, is knowledge at all possible?

This was the question I wrote a short essay about during my student years, and which Bohr read and agreed with.
