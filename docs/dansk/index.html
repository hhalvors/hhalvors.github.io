Ludvig Holberg (1684–1754)

Bergen-born, Copenhagen-based playwright, historian, essayist and professor — the founder of modern Danish-Norwegian literature and, for Høffding, the founder of independent philosophical reflection in Denmark: »Danske Filosofer« (1909) opens with him. Holberg is no system-builder but a skeptic and empiricist in the Montaigne–Bayle vein, hostile to dogma and speculative systems, insisting that "man må først lære at tvivle førend man må lære at tro" (one must first learn to doubt before one can learn to believe). His relevance to the line running toward Bohr's "epistemological lesson" is one of disposition rather than doctrine: epistemic humility, the testing of received norms against sound reason, and — especially in »Niels Klim« — a sustained play with the relativity of standpoint, the recognition that how things appear depends on the observer's position. This anti-dogmatic, perspective-conscious temper is the seedbed of the critical Danish tradition that Høffding inherits and transmits. Further works not listed below include »Introduction til Naturens og Folke-Rettens Kundskab« (1716), the »Moralske Fabler« (1751), and the Latin autobiographical epistles (1728–1743).

Moralske Tanker
1744. Kjøbenhavn
Holberg's central philosophical work: a collection of reflective essays, framed as commentaries on his own epigrams, on morality, religion, knowledge and doubt. The clearest statement of his anti-systematic skepticism and the "doubt before belief" method that Høffding takes as the starting point of Danish philosophy.

Niels Klims underjordiske Rejse (Nicolai Klimii iter subterraneum)
1741. Latin original, Copenhagen/Leipzig; Danish transl. J. Baggesen 1789
Satirical-philosophical subterranean voyage in Latin. Its device — a traveler judging human institutions from wholly other standpoints, and being judged in turn — is a sustained exercise in the relativity of viewpoint and religious/intellectual tolerance: the "epistemological lesson" theme (how things appear depends on the observer's position) in a literary key.

Niels Treschow (1751–1833)

Norwegian-born philosopher; professor of philosophy at Copenhagen 1803–1813, then the first professor of philosophy at the new Frederik University in Christiania (Oslo) from 1813. A Spinoza- and Leibniz-influenced monist and self-described Neoplatonist whose late "Identitets-system" treats the mental and the bodily — and, more broadly, the ideal and the real, subject and object — as two sides of one reality. His insistence that "in the real world only individuals exist" (universals being mere "Hjælpemidler til Oversyn," aids to survey, not knowledge of things in themselves) makes him an early source of the emphasis on concrete individuality that runs through nineteenth-century Danish philosophy. The relevance to the Bohr line is direct: Harald Høffding — Bohr's philosophical mentor, and himself in this catalog — discussed Treschow at length in »Danske Filosofer« (1909) and reprinted his essay on individuality in »Udvalgte Stykker af dansk filosofisk Litteratur« (1910). Treschow is thus the earliest figure on the Danish subject–object / individuality tradition that reaches Bohr's "epistemological lesson." Further works not listed below include the two early critiques of Kant (1794, 1798), »Moral for Folk og Stat« (1810–11), »Lovgivnings-Principier« (1820–23), and »Christendommens Aand« (1828).

Om Gud, Idee- og Sandseverdenen samt de førstes Aabenbarelse i den sidste. Et philosophisk Testament
1831–1833. Christiania (Grøndahl), 3 vols.
Treschow's summa, written in retirement and prefaced with an autobiography; the detailed statement of his monist "Identitets-system." God is not the sum of things but "a real individual" that makes all things one; consciousness "pictures the Absolute One," standing to the manifold as the mind stands to its own thoughts and decisions. The most explicit source for the subject–object identity theme. NB: the third word is »Sandse-« (world of sense), not »Sandheds-«.

Om den menneskelige Natur i Almindelighed, især dens aandelige Sider
1812. Kjøbenhavn
The first psychology written in Danish. Anti-Cartesian: man is not a composite of soul and body; rather the mental and the bodily are the same thing considered as an object of the inner and the outer sense. The clearest early anticipation of the "conditions of observation" theme — the same reality under two irreducible modes of access.

Gives der noget Begreb eller nogen Idee om enslige Ting? Besvaret med Hensyn til Menneskeværd og Menneskevel
1810. Det Kgl. Danske Videnskabernes Selskabs Skrifter, bd. 5
Short, self-contained treatise arguing that only individuals are real and that universals are mere aids to survey, not exact knowledge of things in themselves. The core statement of Treschow's primacy of the individual — the theme most resonant with Bohr's language of the "individuality" (indivisibility) of the quantum phenomenon. Reprinted in excerpt by Høffding, »Udvalgte Stykker af dansk filosofisk Litteratur« (1910), and complete in A.H. Winsnes' ed. of »Philosophiske Forsøg og andre Skrifter« (1966).

Hans Christian Ørsted (1777–1851)

Physicist, chemist and natural philosopher; professor at Copenhagen from 1806 and founder of the Polytekniske Læreanstalt (1829). In 1820 he discovered electromagnetism — that an electric current deflects a magnetic needle — and in 1825 was the first to isolate metallic aluminium. But Ørsted was as much a philosopher of nature as an experimenter: a student of Kant and of the German Naturphilosophie, he held that the same reason the mind finds in itself is objectively at work in nature, so that the laws of thought and the laws of nature ultimately coincide. This dynamical monism — opposed forces as convertible expressions of one underlying activity — is the thesis of his late essay-collection »Aanden i Naturen« (1850), "the spirit (reason) in nature," and it is why he belongs in this catalog: it is a Danish source of the unity-of-nature theme running toward Bohr. Ørsted also gave physics the term »Tankeeksperiment« (thought experiment) and coined much of the modern Danish scientific vocabulary; Høffding treated him among the »Danske Filosofer«. Two English editions cover most of his important work: »The Soul in Nature« (trans. L. & J. B. Horner, London: Bohn, 1852) for the philosophy of nature, and the 83-item »Selected Scientific Works of Hans Christian Ørsted« (trans. Jelved, Jackson & Knudsen, Princeton, 1998) for the scientific and aesthetic essays — the source of most of the translation links below. Further works not listed below include »Videnskaben om Naturens almindelige Love« (1809), »Naturlærens mechaniske Deel« (1844), and the poem »Luftskibet« (1836).

Dissertatio de forma metaphysices elementaris naturae externae
1799. Kjøbenhavn (doctoral dissertation, based on Kant)
Ørsted's Kantian doctoral dissertation, the metaphysical foundation of everything that followed: nature construed through fundamental dynamical forces rather than inert matter. Rendered into English in the Selected Scientific Works as two 1799 pieces — "Fundamentals of the Metaphysics of Nature, Partly According to a New Plan" and "Dissertation on the Structure of the Elementary Metaphysics of External Nature."

Essays on light and the physics of beauty (»det Skjønnes Physik«)
1842–1843. Copenhagen

F.C. Sibbern (1785–1872)

Frederik Christian Sibbern, professor of philosophy at Copenhagen 1813–1870. Close friend of Poul Martin Møller; Kierkegaard attended his lectures. A transitional figure who resisted the Hegelian campaign of the 1830s and anticipated several of Kierkegaard's central arguments.

Forberedelser til en Afhandling om Affectation
1837. Efterladte Skrifter, bd. 3, ed. F.C. Olsen, C.A. Reitzel
Fragment (Brudstykke IV): materials toward a projected treatise on affectation — one of Møller's signature ethical-psychological concepts (inauthenticity, self-deception). Opens with a connected treatment (begun 1837) defining affectation as a compound of falsity and self-deception and distinguishing three degrees (momentary, fixed, changing); then the editor's thematic grouping of the author's scattered aphoristic notes, rich in cultural diagnosis. Contains the striking "two sides" passage — "Da enhver Sag kan sees fra to Sider, var det tilfældigt, fra hvilken Side den øieblikkeligen blev seet" — and the China frog-bundle image. Pairs directly with § 29 of the Moralphilosophi lectures. Transcribed from the Fraktur setting of Efterladte Skrifter vol. 3 (3rd ed.), printed pp. 163–188.

Om Begrebet Ironie
1835–1837. Efterladte Skrifter, bd. 3, ed. F.C. Olsen, C.A. Reitzel
Fragment (Brudstykke II) occasioned by Provost Tryde's review of Sibbern's Æsthetik (Maanedsskrift for Litteratur, bd. 13, 1835). As a member of the journal's editorial board Møller began appended remarks on the concept of irony but, per the editor's note, "bragte kun Indledningen til Ende": only the treatment of MORAL irony was completed, and the piece breaks off as it turns to poetic irony ("Vi gaae nu over til den poetiske Ironie"). Traces irony from Greek moral consciousness (Aristotle) through Kant and Fichte to F. Schlegel's Lucinde and Hegel's "subjectivity that knows itself as the highest" — a direct line to Kierkegaard's Concept of Irony. Forelæsnings-Paragrapher over Moralphilosophien
1837. Efterladte Skrifter, bd. 5, C.A. Reitzel, 1855–56, pp. 141–162
Møller's ethics in 47 numbered lecture-paragraphs (§§), reconstructed by the editor "from lecture-notes of 1837, collated with the Author's manuscript" (F.C. Olsen). The systematic practical-philosophy counterpart to the Ontologien. Three main parts: the Doctrine of Imputation (§§ 6–12; freedom against both indifferentism and determinism, the anatomy of the voluntary/intentional/deliberate act); the Doctrine of Happiness (§§ 13–22; interest, feeling, the Cyrenaic/ Cynic/Epicurean sequence, self-preservation, benevolence, right, the perfect existence); and the Doctrine of the Good (§§ 23–47), covering Virtue (the mean between extremes, affectation), Duty (the categorical imperative), and Conscience — closing (§ 47) with the individual fulfilling its vocation as a member of the rationally organised State. Hans Brøchner (1820–1875)

Professor of philosophy at Copenhagen 1857–1875. Post-Hegelian, moved toward a naturalistic humanism under the influence of Feuerbach and Strauss. His critical engagement with Kierkegaard and Nielsen makes him a crucial third voice; Høffding credited Brøchner's lectures as the direct cause of his own conversion away from Nielsen's position.

Om Søren Kierkegaards Virksomhed som religieus Forfatter
1855. Fædrelandet, 1 December 1855
Published anonymously under the mark "-r". Brøchner's first comprehensive account of Kierkegaard's authorship, written just after Kierkegaard's death. Article text reprinted in C.H. Koch (ed.), Brøchner og Kierkegaard (Scientia Danica, Series H, Humanistica 8, vol. 13).

David Favrholdt (1931–2012)

Dr.phil. and professor of philosophy at the University of Southern Denmark. As a student he heard Niels Bohr lecture on quantum mechanics and atomic physics, an encounter that shaped his philosophical work for the rest of his life. The leading Danish interpreter of Bohr's philosophy of complementarity and of the epistemological limits set by language and concepts.

Filosoffen Niels Bohr
2009. Informations Forlag, Copenhagen
Favrholdt's synthesis of his lifelong study of Bohr as thinker, arguing that Bohr was a genuine philosopher whose insight into quantum mechanics yielded a modern understanding of the limits of thought imposed by our language and concepts.
